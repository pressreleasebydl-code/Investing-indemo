## 1. What Are Debt Investments?

A **debt investment** is any financial instrument in which you — the investor — act as the lender. Instead of buying a stake in a company (equity), you lend money to a borrower and receive it back with interest over an agreed timeframe. The core exchange is simple: capital today in return for a contractual promise of repayment plus yield tomorrow.

Debt investments are sometimes called *fixed income investments* or *debt securities*, although these terms are not perfectly interchangeable. Fixed income specifically implies a predictable, scheduled interest payment. Broader debt investments — including the discounted non-performing loan (NPL) model explored later in this guide — may instead deliver a lump-sum profit at the end of a recovery process rather than monthly coupon payments.

> **Key definition:** In a debt investment, you are the creditor. Your return comes from interest payments, capital recovery, or profit-sharing — not from price appreciation of an ownership stake. Your claim on assets ranks above equity holders if the borrower defaults.

Debt investments sit at the core of most diversified portfolios. They offer relatively predictable income, seniority over equity in case of default, and — in the case of secured debt — collateral protection. But not all debt investments carry the same risk or return profile. The spectrum runs from near-risk-free government bonds yielding 3–4% to high-yield distressed debt strategies targeting 15–40% annually.

## 2. Types of Debt Investments

Understanding the landscape of debt investments helps you position any opportunity correctly on the risk/return spectrum.

### Government Bonds (Sovereign Debt)
Issued by national governments, these are the safest debt investments available. US Treasuries, German Bunds, and similar instruments are backed by the full taxing power of the state. Yields are low (typically 2–5% in developed markets in 2026) but liquidity is excellent and default risk is minimal for investment-grade issuers.

### Municipal Bonds
Issued by local and regional governments to fund infrastructure, schools, and public projects. Municipal bonds often carry tax advantages — in the US, interest is typically exempt from federal income tax. Returns are modest but tax-adjusted yields can be competitive.

### Corporate Bonds
Companies issue corporate bonds to fund operations or acquisitions. Investment-grade corporate bonds (rated BBB– or higher) offer moderate yields. High-yield ("junk") bonds carry substantially more credit risk but can deliver returns of 6–12% or more.

### Certificates of Deposit (CDs)
Bank-issued debt instruments with a fixed term and guaranteed interest rate. CDs are FDIC-insured (up to limits) in the US, making them extremely low risk. They are illiquid during the term and returns rarely beat inflation significantly.

### Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)
Pools of mortgage loans packaged into tradeable securities. Investors receive a share of the interest and principal payments made by thousands of individual borrowers. Returns depend on prepayment rates and credit quality of the underlying loans.

### Peer-to-Peer (P2P) and Peer-to-Business (P2B) Lending
Online platforms match individual investors directly with borrowers — consumer, business, or real estate — bypassing traditional banks. Returns of 8–15% are common. The P2P market has matured significantly since 2015 and now includes regulated platforms operating under MiFID II and similar frameworks across Europe.

### Discounted Debt Investments (Non-Performing Loans)
The most sophisticated — and highest-potential-return — category. Distressed or defaulted loans (typically mortgages) are purchased from banks at a steep discount to face value. Specialized servicers then recover value through legal proceedings, negotiated settlements, or asset sales. This is the model that powers **Indemo**.

## 3. Traditional vs. Alternative Debt Investments

The divide between traditional and alternative debt investments has grown enormously important for retail investors in the 2020s. Traditional debt — government bonds, corporate bonds, CDs — is purchased through brokerage accounts, ETFs, or mutual funds. It is liquid, transparent, and highly standardized.

Alternative debt investments include private credit, direct lending, distressed debt, and mortgage-backed P2P notes. Until recently, these were exclusively the domain of institutional investors: hedge funds, private equity firms, and insurance companies with minimum ticket sizes of millions of euros. The returns were correspondingly higher — often 15–25% annually.

Platforms like **Indemo** exist precisely to close this gap. By pooling small investor contributions into Notes backed by individual mortgage claims, Indemo democratizes access to the Spanish NPL market — a segment that was previously reserved for institutional buyers.

> **Why does this gap exist?** Banks hold portfolios of non-performing mortgage loans on their balance sheets. Keeping them there is expensive (regulatory capital requirements). Banks prefer to sell these portfolios in bulk — at a discount — to specialized buyers. Historically, only firms with €50M+ could participate. Indemo changes the minimum to €10.

## 5. How Indemo's Discounted Debt Model Works

The discounted debt investment model is the most distinctive — and most profitable — feature of Indemo. Here is how your money works:

**Step 1 — Spanish banks sell non-performing mortgages**
When a borrower defaults on their mortgage, the loan becomes a non-performing loan (NPL). Spanish banks sell these portfolios at significant discounts — sometimes 30–70 cents on the euro — to clear their balance sheets.

**Step 2 — Tamarindo Vector acquires the claims**
Indemo's origination partner, Tamarindo Vector S.L. (licensed by the Bank of Spain under Act 5/2019), purchases individual mortgage claims from these portfolios. Each claim is backed by a specific Spanish property used as collateral.

**Step 3 — Indemo packages the claim into a Note**
The acquired debt claim is structured into an investable "Note" listed on the Indemo platform. Each Note corresponds to a single debt claim (updated November 2025), allowing full transparency on the underlying asset.

**Step 4 — You invest from €10**
Retail investors fund the Note starting from just €10. The Note dashboard shows the property location, Price-to-Value ratio, appraisal reports, and legal recovery stage.

**Step 5 — Taurus Ibérica works the recovery**
An independent servicer — Taurus Ibérica — manages the legal recovery process: negotiating with debtors, pursuing court enforcement, or selling the claim on the secondary NPL market at a profit.

**Step 6 — Profits split 50/50**
Once recovery is complete, all costs (legal fees, servicer commissions, platform fee of 5–6%) are deducted first. The remaining profit is divided equally between investors and Tamarindo. Your principal is returned together with your share of the profit.

> **Example:** A mortgage originally worth €200,000 is purchased at a discount for €60,000. After legal proceedings, the property sells at auction for €140,000. After costs (€15,000), the net profit of €65,000 is split: €32,500 to investors and €32,500 to Tamarindo. On a €10,000 investment that formed part of the Note, an investor might recover their capital plus a return of 30–40% depending on the recovery timeline.

### Mortgage Loans (the second product)
Alongside discounted debt, Indemo offers standard mortgage loans originated by Tamarindo — primarily for secondary residences in Spain. These earn a fixed interest rate of around 10% per annum. Lower risk and more predictable than DDIs, but with commensurately lower upside.

## 7. Risks to Understand Before Investing

Indemo is explicitly classified as a **high-risk investment**. Every investor should understand these risks clearly before committing capital.

### 🔒 Illiquidity Risk
There is currently no secondary market on Indemo. Once you invest in a Note, your capital is locked until the underlying debt is resolved. Recovery timelines are uncertain — the platform advertises a typical horizon of 24 months, but some recoveries extend to 3–5 years. Only invest money you will not need in the short or medium term.

### ⚙️ Servicer Concentration Risk
The entire recovery process depends on a single servicer: Taurus Ibérica. If this servicer encounters operational or financial difficulties, there is no backup recovery mechanism currently in place. This is the most significant structural risk on the platform.

### 🌍 Geographic Concentration
All investments are backed by Spanish real estate. A downturn in the Spanish property market, changes to Spanish insolvency law, or disruptions to the court system would affect all Notes simultaneously. This is not a globally diversified platform.

### 💸 Cost Erosion in Extended Recoveries
Because costs are deducted before profit-sharing, prolonged recoveries erode investor returns. Legal fees, servicer costs, and platform commissions accumulate over time. A deal that looks attractive at 18 months may deliver significantly compressed returns at 48 months.

### 🌱 Platform Early-Stage Risk
Indemo was founded in 2022 and launched in 2023. Revenue reached €1.02 million in 2025, nearly tripling from the prior year — but the platform is not yet profitable at the operating level. This is a genuine early-stage platform, not a mature institution.

### ⚖️ Regulatory and Legal Risk
Changes to MiFID II, Spanish real estate lending law, or Latvian financial regulations could affect how the platform operates. The investor compensation scheme covers platform insolvency (90% of losses up to €20,000) but does not protect against investment losses.
